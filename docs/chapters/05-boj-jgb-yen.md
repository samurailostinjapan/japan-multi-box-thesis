# 5. BOJ, JGBs, and the Yen

The BOJ faces a practical rate ceiling.

Japan's firms, households, banks, and public finances adapted to decades of near-zero rates. As a result, relatively low policy rates can produce meaningful stress.

A rate increase can transmit through:

```text
higher policy rate
→ higher lending and market funding cost
→ weaker capex
→ defensive cash retention
→ lower bonus and hiring capacity
→ weaker household demand
→ more political pressure for support
```

The difficulty is that the same rate increase may still be too small to restore durable yen confidence.

That creates an **insufficient-hike yen regime**: rates rise enough to hurt the domestic economy but not enough to reverse the currency trend.

FX intervention can stop disorderly speculation. It cannot create a new regime by itself.

Without fiscal or monetary follow-through, intervention becomes:

```text
yen rally
→ speculative positions reduced
→ policy relief fades
→ structural weakness returns
→ market reloads the trade
```

Japan also faces a long-yield constraint. If JGB yields rise too far, debt-service expectations and fiscal credibility deteriorate.

The BOJ may therefore be pressured to raise short rates while also containing long yields.

That is the **dual-rate ceiling**: tighter policy for firms and households, but partial rate suppression for the sovereign.

The yen remains the system's pressure valve because the alternatives—aggressive rate hikes, major fiscal tightening, or unrestricted bond buying—each create larger problems elsewhere.
