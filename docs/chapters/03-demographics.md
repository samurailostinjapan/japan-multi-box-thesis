# 3. Demographic Arithmetic

Japan's birth problem is usually discussed through the fertility rate. The more important long-run constraint is the number of women in childbearing cohorts.

Even if the fertility rate improves, a smaller population of potential mothers limits the number of births.

The transmission mechanism is wider than biology:

- later marriage;
- later first births;
- infertility and age-related decline;
- low sexual frequency;
- housing and childcare costs;
- fear of being unable to provide a full middle-class childhood;
- work and care responsibilities;
- reduced confidence in future income and prices.

This creates **fertility conversion failure**: the desire for children does not convert into actual births.

The 2030 period matters because the cohort arithmetic becomes increasingly difficult to offset. Policy may improve childcare access or subsidize births, but those measures cannot fully replace missing cohorts.

Immigration can reduce labor shortages, but Japan competes globally for workers. Weak yen wages, rising living costs, higher fees, and residency friction reduce retention.

The demographic issue therefore becomes a combined fertility, labor, migration, and care-capacity problem.
