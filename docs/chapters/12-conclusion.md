# 12. Counterarguments and Conclusion

## Main counterarguments

### Japan borrows in its own currency

Correct. The thesis is not based on involuntary nominal default.

The relevant costs are inflation, currency depreciation, rising annual interest expense, financial repression, redistribution, and weaker policy flexibility.

### Most JGBs are domestically held

Correct, but domestic institutions have balance-sheet and fiduciary constraints. Marginal investors still influence the clearing price of new issuance.

### The BOJ can buy bonds

Correct, but large-scale buying affects yen credibility, inflation expectations, market functioning, political legitimacy, and consistency with rate hikes.

### Japan has large foreign assets

Correct, but those assets are dispersed across institutions and private holders. Repatriation is slow, governed, and not automatically available for fiscal stabilization.

### Corporate Japan has large cash balances

Correct, but cash can remain defensive rather than productive.

### AI and semiconductor investment can raise growth

Correct. This is the strongest upside path. The unresolved question is whether the gains spread into domestic wages, services, SMEs, and tax capacity quickly enough.

## Falsification

The thesis should lose confidence if Japan achieves several years of broad real wage growth, stronger consumption without recurring relief, broad capex above the expected rate ceiling, stable JGB yields despite higher issuance, durable yen strength, flattening debt-service projections, improved fertility relative to cohort projections, stronger foreign-worker retention, lower regional bankruptcy and credit losses, and better care-delivery capacity.

## Conclusion

Japan is not running out of tools.

It is running into the interactions among those tools.

The country can continue borrowing, intervening, subsidizing, tightening, supporting firms, and reallocating capital. The cost is that each measure reduces flexibility somewhere else.

The most likely outcome is not sudden collapse. It is slow compression: more expensive stability, weaker household surplus, selective industrial winners, more conditional social protection, higher regional pressure, reduced shock absorption, and greater reliance on the yen as the adjustment valve.

> **Japan's problem is not that it has no tools. Its problem is that using one tool increasingly damages the conditions required for the others to work.**
