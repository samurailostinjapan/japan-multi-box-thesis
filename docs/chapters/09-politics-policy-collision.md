# 9. Politics and Policy Collision

Political incentives push Japan toward maintenance spending.

When households face higher food, energy, housing, medical, and tax burdens, the government has strong incentives to provide subsidies, tax cuts, cash benefits, credit guarantees, energy support, medical exemptions, and industrial assistance.

These measures can be necessary. The problem is that they often reduce future policy space.

A temporary tax cut becomes hard to reverse. A targeted benefit becomes a permanent expectation. A corporate liquidity program delays closures but can preserve weak firms. Bond buying reduces yields but can weaken the yen.

This is the **solution-collision spiral**.

Large electoral mandates do not remove the problem. They can increase political ownership of outcomes.

As approval weakens, the incentive for visible relief rises. That can produce more spending precisely when JGB markets are becoming more sensitive to fiscal credibility.

The political system therefore becomes part of the transmission mechanism, not an external solution to it.
