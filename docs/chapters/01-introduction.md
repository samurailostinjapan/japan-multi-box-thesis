# 1. Introduction

Japan is usually discussed through separate policy debates: debt sustainability, population decline, BOJ normalization, the weak yen, household inflation, or political stimulus. This thesis argues that the separation is misleading.

The central problem is **interaction density**.

Japan's debt does not become dangerous only when the state cannot borrow. It becomes constraining when higher refinancing costs compete with social security, healthcare, household relief, industrial policy, and regional support.

The BOJ does not need to lose control completely for monetary policy to become ineffective. It only needs to reach a point where rates high enough to stabilize the yen are also high enough to weaken investment, bonuses, SMEs, and the national budget.

The purpose of this thesis is therefore to explain how Japan's remaining buffers interact, where they are weakening, and why the years around 2030 represent a convergence window rather than a single-trigger crisis date.

## Main research question

How do demographic decline, debt rollover, social spending, constrained monetary policy, weak-yen import inflation, corporate behavior, household retrenchment, and political incentives combine to produce slow structural compression?

## Contribution

The thesis contributes three ideas:

1. Japan's main constraint is policy interaction rather than one headline variable.
2. Household surplus is a better measure of social stability than nominal income alone.
3. The state can remain fully functional while its shock-absorption capacity deteriorates.
