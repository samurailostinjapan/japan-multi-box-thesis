# 8. Healthcare and Elderly Care

Japan's aging problem is moving from finance into operating capacity.

A growing share of home-care households already consists of an elderly caregiver looking after an elderly recipient.

The arrangement remains inexpensive for the state only while the caregiver remains functional.

The failure path is:

```text
elderly caregiver supports elderly patient
→ caregiver health deteriorates
→ caregiver becomes second patient
→ household loses informal-care capacity
→ adult children supply money or labor
→ public care and medical demand rises
```

This is the **dual-dependency household**.

Higher patient out-of-pocket caps are one response to rising medical costs. They protect the insurance system by transferring more of the burden to households at the moment of illness.

The state may save on the immediate claim, but a larger household shock can accelerate savings drawdown, treatment delay, caregiver failure, and demand for public support.

The long-run constraint is not only whether Japan can budget for care. It is whether enough workers, facilities, municipalities, transport systems, and family caregivers exist to deliver it.
