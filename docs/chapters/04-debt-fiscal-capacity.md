# 4. Debt and Fiscal Capacity

## The level is not the danger. The reset is.

Japan's public debt is usually introduced with one number: the debt-to-GDP ratio. That number matters, but it is not the mechanism that will determine how fiscal pressure enters the economy over the next decade.

Japan is not approaching a conventional foreign-currency funding crisis. It issues debt in yen, has a deep domestic financial system, retains a large tax base, and has a central bank capable of purchasing government securities. Those facts make a sudden nominal default unlikely. They do not make the debt costless.

The relevant pressure enters through the annual budget.

Old debt issued at very low interest rates matures. Refunding bonds replace much of it. New deficits require additional issuance. The average coupon on the outstanding stock rises slowly because the debt has a long maturity. Interest expenditure then accelerates with a lag. Once that expenditure is embedded in the budget, it competes directly with social security, healthcare, long-term care, local transfers, defense, industrial policy, household relief, and every new political promise.

The core proposition of this chapter is therefore:

> **Japan's debt constraint is not primarily an inability to issue yen. It is the progressive conversion of fiscal flexibility into debt-service obligation as the existing stock is refinanced at higher rates.**

This is a rollover problem, a budget-composition problem, and ultimately a policy-capacity problem.

The chapter does not predict a sudden sovereign default. It explains why Japan can continue borrowing successfully while the quality of fiscal policy deteriorates. The system remains funded, but an increasing share of revenue is pre-committed before the government begins making new policy choices.

---

## 4.1 Why debt-to-GDP is necessary but insufficient

The debt-to-GDP ratio measures the size of public liabilities relative to the economy expected to support them. It is useful for international comparison, for evaluating the long-run relationship between debt accumulation and nominal growth, and for identifying how little margin Japan has if interest rates rise above growth for a sustained period.

It is not sufficient for explaining timing.

A debt ratio can remain broadly stable while the annual interest bill rises. It can also decline temporarily because nominal GDP is inflated by higher prices even as household purchasing power weakens and the government's refinancing cost increases. Conversely, a high debt ratio can coexist with low immediate pressure when the average interest rate is close to zero and maturities are long.

The timing of fiscal compression depends on four variables:

1. the amount of debt that must be refinanced;
2. the interest rates attached to new and refinanced securities;
3. the maturity structure of the outstanding stock;
4. the amount of new borrowing required to finance the annual gap between expenditure and revenue.

The debt stock is the reservoir. Rollover determines how quickly current market rates flow into the budget.

Japan's Ministry of Finance itself separates the stock indicator from the flow indicators. Its public-finance material distinguishes gross debt-to-GDP, the overall fiscal balance, and the primary balance. The overall fiscal balance asks whether taxes and other ordinary revenue cover both policy expenditure and interest. The primary balance excludes interest payments.[^mof-fact-sheet]

That distinction is essential because a primary surplus does not mean the debt stock stops rising.

A government can balance policy expenditure against revenue while still borrowing to pay interest. If the interest bill grows faster than the primary surplus, total debt continues to increase. This is exactly what appears in the Ministry of Finance's own medium-term budget projections.

The debt ratio is therefore the strategic background. The annual fiscal balance is the operating constraint.

## 4.2 The FY2026 budget already shows the collision

The FY2026 initial general-account budget totals ¥122.3 trillion. It contains:

| FY2026 initial budget | Trillion yen |
|---|---:|
| Social-security expenditure | 39.1 |
| National debt service | 31.3 |
| Local allocation tax grants, etc. | 20.9 |
| Other general expenditure | 31.1 |
| **Total expenditure** | **122.3** |
| Tax revenue | 83.7 |
| Other revenue | 9.0 |
| Government bond issuance | 29.6 |

The initial budget therefore relies on new financial-resource bonds for approximately 24.2% of total expenditure. Special deficit-financing bonds account for ¥22.9 trillion of the ¥29.6 trillion, while construction bonds account for ¥6.7 trillion.[^mof-budget-2026]

Three features matter.

First, national debt service is already roughly one-quarter of the entire general-account budget. It is not a distant future problem waiting to begin.

Second, debt service and social security together total approximately ¥70.3 trillion. That is about 84% of projected tax revenue. This does not mean those two categories are literally funded only from taxes while all other expenditure is debt-financed; government accounting is not earmarked in that manner. It does show the scale of inherited obligations relative to the most stable revenue source.

Third, the government still needs almost ¥30 trillion of new bonds in a year for which it presents the initial general-account primary balance as slightly positive. The apparent contradiction disappears once interest is restored to the calculation.

The FY2026 budget can claim progress under a primary-balance definition while the full fiscal balance remains negative.

That is not an accounting trick. The primary balance is a legitimate analytical measure. The problem is political presentation. When interest costs were low, movement toward primary balance could plausibly signal eventual debt stabilization. When interest costs are rising rapidly, a primary surplus can coexist with a worsening total balance.

Japan is now moving into that second regime.

## 4.3 What the national debt-service line contains

The national debt-service figure is not simply the coupon payment on outstanding JGBs.

In the FY2026 initial budget, the ¥31.3 trillion total consists of approximately:

- ¥18.2 trillion in debt-redemption expenses;
- ¥13.0 trillion in interest-payment and discount expenses;
- a small administrative component.

Within the interest category, JGB interest accounts for approximately ¥12.8 trillion. The FY2026 interest and discount line is about ¥2.5 trillion higher than in the FY2025 initial budget.[^mof-debt-report]

The redemption component requires care.

Japan uses a Government Debt Consolidation Fund and a 60-year redemption rule for construction bonds and special deficit-financing bonds. Under that framework, the general account transfers an amount linked to 1.6% of the relevant outstanding debt to the special account. Maturing securities are redeemed in cash from the investor's perspective, but much of the required funding is raised through refunding bonds. The legal and accounting framework is designed to retire the original debt over a 60-year period rather than requiring the full principal to disappear at each maturity.[^mof-sixty-year]

This matters because gross issuance is much larger than the amount of bonds used to finance new policy.

The FY2026 issuance plan involves approximately ¥180.7 trillion of JGBs when refunding and other categories are included. Calendar-based market issuance is set at ¥168.5 trillion. By contrast, the general-account budget shows ¥29.6 trillion of new financial-resource bonds.[^mof-issuance-plan]

The market must therefore absorb far more than the annual headline deficit.

Refunding does not mechanically increase the outstanding principal when one maturing bond is replaced by another. It does, however, reset the cost attached to that principal. The refinancing transaction is neutral for the face value of the debt stock but not for future interest expenditure.

This is why the project's central line is:

> **The level is not the danger. The reset is.**

A ¥100 unit of debt issued near zero and a ¥100 unit of debt issued at 2% have the same face value. They do not impose the same annual fiscal burden.

## 4.4 The long maturity buys time, not immunity

Japan has deliberately extended the maturity of its debt.

The stock-based average maturity reached approximately 9.5 years at the end of FY2025. The weighted-average interest rate on general bonds remained only 0.98% in FY2025 despite the sharp rise in market yields. General-bond interest payments were ¥9.4 trillion in FY2025, while the FY2026 budget places them at ¥13.0 trillion.[^mof-maturity]

The long maturity is a major stabilizer.

It prevents current market rates from applying immediately to the entire debt stock. It spreads the reset over years. It gives the government time to adjust taxes, expenditure, issuance composition, and monetary policy. It reduces the probability of a sudden cash-flow shock.

It also creates a dangerous optical delay.

Because the average coupon moves slowly, policymakers can observe a large rise in market yields while the budget still reflects a much lower average funding cost. The gap can encourage the belief that the system has absorbed normalization successfully. In reality, the interest bill is still in transit.

The lag works in two stages.

### Stage one: the market reprices

New five-, ten-, twenty-, and thirty-year securities clear at higher yields. Auction results and secondary-market pricing establish the new marginal cost of funding.

### Stage two: the budget catches up

As more low-coupon debt matures and more new deficits are financed at the new rate structure, the weighted-average coupon rises. The annual interest bill then incorporates the market repricing.

The average maturity controls the speed of transmission. It does not cancel the transmission.

A long maturity is therefore both a strength and a delay mechanism. It makes Japan more resilient than a country funded mainly through short-term debt, but it also allows fiscal pressure to accumulate beneath a stable surface.

## 4.5 The refinancing base is still expanding

The problem would be easier if Japan were refinancing a fixed stock of debt while running no new deficits.

It is not.

General bonds outstanding rose from ¥830.6 trillion in FY2016 to ¥1,104.3 trillion in FY2025. The FY2026 initial-budget basis places the amount at approximately ¥1,145.4 trillion.[^mof-maturity]

The debt base is therefore growing while the cost of refinancing it is rising.

The mechanism is:

```text
higher yields
→ higher interest expenditure
→ larger full fiscal deficit
→ more new bond issuance
→ larger debt stock
→ more future refinancing
```

This is the debt-service ratchet.

It does not require investor panic. It can operate under orderly auctions, continued domestic demand, and a functioning yen market. Each annual budget merely begins from a slightly less favorable position.

The distinction between new financial-resource bonds and refunding bonds is important but should not obscure the combined burden.

New financial-resource bonds increase the debt stock because they finance spending not covered by current revenue. Refunding bonds replace maturing obligations. Together they determine the scale, maturity, and price of the sovereign's market presence.

Japan can reduce one year's new-bond headline and still face heavy gross issuance. It can lengthen maturities and reduce near-term refunding risk while paying a higher term premium. It can shorten issuance to lower immediate cost while increasing future rollover exposure.

There is no maturity structure that removes the tradeoff.

## 4.6 The FY2026 budget openly acknowledges the reset

The Ministry of Finance raised the interest-rate assumption used for the FY2026 budget calculation from 2.0% to 3.0%.

It explained the resulting increase in two parts:

- approximately ¥1.0 trillion from the higher rate applied to scheduled issuance;
- approximately ¥1.5 trillion from the replacement of outstanding bonds with higher-interest issues while the debt balance continues to rise.[^mof-budget-rate]

This is unusually clean confirmation of the thesis mechanism.

The first component is the current-flow effect: debt issued during the budget year costs more.

The second component is the stock-reset effect: old securities are replaced by more expensive ones.

The budget assumption was deliberately conservative relative to the recent one-month average of the long-term rate at the time, which the Ministry reported at 1.9%. The 3.0% assumption included protection against a sharp increase.

By July 2026, however, the ten-year JGB yield had already reached approximately 2.9%, while twenty- and thirty-year yields had moved close to 3.9% and 4.0% respectively during a period of inflation and fiscal concern.[^reuters-yields-july]

The important observation is not that every bond is being issued at 3% or 4%. Different maturities have different yields, and the budget rate is an accounting assumption rather than a direct representation of the entire curve.

The observation is that the stress allowance is no longer remote from the market.

The budget has moved from protecting against a hypothetical rate shock to operating near the level once treated as that protection.

## 4.7 Official projections already cross the ¥40 trillion line

The Ministry of Finance provides two mechanical projections for the effect of the FY2026 budget through FY2029.

### Case 1: 3.0% nominal economic growth

| Trillion yen | FY2026 | FY2027 | FY2028 | FY2029 |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| National debt service | 31.3 | 34.7 | 38.0 | 41.3 |
| Interest payments | 13.0 | 15.5 | 18.5 | 21.6 |
| Social security | 39.1 | 39.8 | 40.4 | 41.0 |
| Tax revenue | 83.7 | 87.8 | 92.2 | 95.5 |
| New bond requirement / balance gap | 29.6 | 33.1 | 34.3 | 36.3 |

### Case 2: 1.5% nominal economic growth

| Trillion yen | FY2026 | FY2027 | FY2028 | FY2029 |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| National debt service | 31.3 | 34.7 | 37.6 | 40.3 |
| Interest payments | 13.0 | 15.4 | 18.1 | 20.7 |
| Social security | 39.1 | 39.7 | 40.3 | 40.7 |
| Tax revenue | 83.7 | 86.3 | 89.0 | 90.6 |
| New bond requirement / balance gap | 29.6 | 34.0 | 35.7 | 38.0 |

The numbers are mechanical projections, not enacted future budgets. They assume continuation of systems and policies underlying the FY2026 budget. They are not forecasts of political behavior or unanticipated shocks.[^mof-projections]

Their significance is nevertheless large.

Under both growth cases, national debt service reaches approximately ¥40–41 trillion in FY2029.

Under the higher-growth case, debt service is actually slightly larger because the underlying rate path is higher. Faster nominal growth raises tax revenue and lowers the required new issuance, but it does not prevent the interest bill from rising.

This directly challenges the simple claim that nominal growth solves the problem automatically.

Nominal growth helps the denominator and supports revenue. Its fiscal benefit depends on what produces the growth and what happens to interest rates. If nominal growth is accompanied by higher inflation, higher bond yields, and new indexed or politically required spending, the improvement is partly absorbed.

The correct relationship is not:

```text
more nominal GDP
→ debt problem solved
```

It is:

```text
more nominal GDP
→ more tax revenue and a larger denominator
but also potentially
→ higher yields, indexed expenditure, and support demands
```

The net result is determined by the interaction among growth, rates, primary spending, and political commitments.

## 4.8 A primary surplus can coexist with a worsening fiscal balance

The higher-growth Ministry of Finance case produces a primary surplus in the general-account calculation:

- approximately ¥1.3 trillion in FY2026;
- ¥1.3 trillion in FY2027;
- ¥3.3 trillion in FY2028;
- ¥4.7 trillion in FY2029.

Yet the same case shows the full general-account fiscal balance worsening from approximately minus ¥11.7 trillion in FY2026 to minus ¥17.0 trillion in FY2029.[^mof-projections]

The reason is interest.

The projected primary surplus improves by ¥3.4 trillion between FY2026 and FY2029. Interest payments increase by ¥8.6 trillion.

The interest increase overwhelms the primary improvement.

This is the point at which fiscal rhetoric can become detached from fiscal capacity.

A primary-balance target remains useful because it isolates current policy choices from inherited interest costs. It can show whether the government is adding to debt before interest. It is also the balance most directly influenced by taxes and discretionary expenditure.

But the bond market funds the total cash requirement, not a political slogan.

Investors care about:

- the total amount of issuance;
- the maturity structure;
- the expected inflation path;
- the BOJ's reaction function;
- the credibility of future taxation and spending;
- the probability that the government will pressure the central bank to suppress yields.

A primary surplus does not eliminate any of those questions if the full deficit is widening.

In a high-debt normalization cycle, the full balance becomes the binding measure.

## 4.9 The official sensitivity analysis points toward ¥50 trillion

The Ministry of Finance also calculates how national debt service changes if interest rates from FY2027 onward differ from its main case.

By FY2029:

- rates 1 percentage point above the main case raise national debt service from ¥41.3 trillion to approximately ¥45.1 trillion;
- rates 2 percentage points above the main case raise it to approximately ¥48.9 trillion;
- rates 1 percentage point below the main case reduce it to approximately ¥37.6 trillion.[^mof-sensitivity]

This provides a disciplined basis for the project's ¥50 trillion stress threshold.

The figure is not a prediction that debt service will definitely reach ¥50 trillion in 2029. It is a sensitivity result showing that the threshold is reachable within the official projection framework if the rate path remains materially above the assumed forward curve.

The distinction matters.

A responsible thesis should not take a stress scenario and relabel it as a baseline. The correct claim is:

> **The government's own sensitivity analysis places debt service near ¥50 trillion by FY2029 under a two-percentage-point upward rate deviation.**

That is enough to make the threshold analytically relevant.

It also shows the nonlinearity of time. The rate shock adds little to the FY2026 budget because the debt stock has not yet rolled. Its effect grows each year:

- no immediate FY2026 difference in the table;
- +¥1.6 trillion in FY2027 under the two-point shock;
- +¥4.2 trillion in FY2028;
- +¥7.6 trillion in FY2029.

Rollover is the multiplier.

The same interest-rate change becomes progressively more expensive as more securities mature into the new regime.

## 4.10 Beyond FY2029, the cash-flow burden keeps climbing

The Ministry's Government Debt Consolidation Fund cash-flow projection extends to FY2035 under a set of mechanical assumptions.

It shows:

| Fiscal year | Covered JGBs outstanding | Interest payments, etc. | Refunding bonds issued |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| FY2026 | ¥1,132.5 tn | ¥13.18 tn | ¥132.28 tn |
| FY2029 | ¥1,182.2 tn | ¥21.77 tn | ¥130.78 tn |
| FY2030 | ¥1,200.0 tn | ¥24.49 tn | ¥134.29 tn |
| FY2033 | ¥1,251.6 tn | ¥31.92 tn | ¥155.12 tn |
| FY2035 | ¥1,285.0 tn | ¥36.05 tn | ¥153.33 tn |

These figures are not directly identical to the general-account national debt-service total. The cash-flow table covers a defined set of JGBs linked to the fixed-rate transfer and excludes several special categories. Its "interest payments, etc." definition also includes specific items beyond coupon expense.[^mof-cashflow]

The direction is still important.

Even under a mechanical framework that holds the FY2029 new-bond requirement and interest rates constant after FY2029, the covered debt stock and interest cash flow continue rising.

That means 2030 is not the end point. It is the point at which the reset becomes politically difficult to hide.

By the early 2030s, the government is no longer dealing only with the cost of the current year's normalization. It is carrying the accumulated effect of several years of refinancing.

The debt system has memory.

## 4.11 Social security and debt service become twin fixed-cost blocks

The fiscal problem becomes clearer when debt service is placed beside social security.

In the Ministry's higher-growth case for FY2029:

- national debt service is ¥41.3 trillion;
- social-security expenditure is ¥41.0 trillion;
- tax revenue is ¥95.5 trillion.

Together, debt service and social security equal approximately 86% of tax revenue.

In the lower-growth case:

- national debt service is ¥40.3 trillion;
- social security is ¥40.7 trillion;
- tax revenue is ¥90.6 trillion.

Together, the two categories equal approximately 89% of tax revenue.

Again, this is not an earmarking statement. It is a fiscal-capacity ratio.

The government must also fund:

- local allocation tax grants;
- defense;
- education;
- public works;
- science and technology;
- childcare;
- industrial policy;
- disaster response;
- energy support;
- regional programs;
- administration.

The collision is therefore not debt versus one discretionary program. It is debt service plus aging-related expenditure versus the full operating state.

This is why the thesis does not describe the 2030s only as a bond-market story.

The bond cost becomes a hospital, municipality, care, household, and corporate-support story because those systems compete for the same fiscal capacity.

## 4.12 Supplementary budgets reveal the political ratchet

Japan's initial budget is not the final annual fiscal position.

Supplementary budgets are routinely used to respond to disasters, inflation, energy prices, security risks, and political pressure. The gap between the initial budget and the eventual fiscal outcome is therefore structurally important.

The first FY2026 supplementary budget added approximately ¥3.11 trillion of general-account expenditure. It restored general contingency reserves and created additional reserves for Middle East-related risks and energy-price effects. The supplementary amount was financed through additional special deficit-financing bonds.[^mof-supplement]

After the supplement:

- general-account expenditure rose from ¥122.3 trillion to approximately ¥125.4 trillion;
- government bond revenue rose from ¥29.6 trillion to approximately ¥32.7 trillion;
- the bond-dependency ratio increased to 26.1%;
- the initial general-account primary surplus shifted to a deficit.

The Ministry adjusted the market issuance schedule so that FY2026 calendar-based market issuance did not have to increase by the full supplementary amount. It did so partly by reducing issuance previously scheduled in the April–June accounting-adjustment period for the prior fiscal year.[^mof-supplement]

That is a useful debt-management operation. It smooths the volume reaching the market in the current calendar year.

It does not mean the fiscal cost vanished.

The general account still incurred additional expenditure financed by special deficit bonds. The adjustment changed the timing and composition of issuance, not the underlying political fact that a new shock created a new debt claim.

This is the supplementary-budget ratchet:

```text
shock or household pressure
→ supplementary support
→ additional deficit bond authorization
→ issuance timing adjusted to preserve market stability
→ debt stock and future service burden remain
```

Japan is very good at smoothing the market expression of fiscal expansion.

That strength can obscure the accumulation of the fiscal obligation itself.

## 4.13 Temporary relief can become permanent fiscal architecture

The same mechanism applies to tax cuts and benefits.

A temporary reduction in the food consumption tax can provide real near-term relief. It can also remove a stable revenue source at the exact point when debt service is accelerating. By mid-2026, the government was considering a two-year reduction of the food tax from 8% to 1%, with estimates placing the annual revenue loss in the multi-trillion-yen range and no settled replacement funding.[^reuters-food-tax]

The fiscal danger is not only the two-year arithmetic.

Temporary relief creates a political reference point. Restoring the old rate later is experienced by households as a tax increase. If prices continue rising, the government may find reversal politically impossible. A temporary measure can therefore become a permanent reduction in structural revenue.

The same applies to income-linked benefits.

A targeted benefit is more efficient than a universal payment if it reaches households with the highest marginal need. It also creates administrative thresholds, political constituencies, and an expectation of continuing support when wages fail to keep pace with unavoidable costs.

The interaction is:

```text
household compression
→ tax relief or benefit
→ lower revenue or higher primary spending
→ larger issuance requirement
→ higher debt-service path
→ less capacity for the next household shock
```

This is not an argument against relief.

It is an argument that relief must be evaluated as part of the debt system rather than as an isolated social policy.

## 4.14 Nominal tax growth can create a false sense of room

Japan's FY2026 initial budget projects record tax revenue of approximately ¥83.7 trillion, up nearly ¥6 trillion from the FY2025 initial budget.[^mof-budget-2026]

Strong nominal revenue can reflect:

- higher nominal wages;
- corporate profits;
- consumption prices;
- asset-related income;
- the end of temporary tax reductions;
- bracket and base effects.

That revenue is real money for the government.

It does not necessarily mean household capacity has improved by the same amount.

When prices rise, consumption-tax receipts can increase while the volume of household consumption weakens. Nominal wages can increase while real disposable income remains compressed by food, housing, energy, insurance premiums, and taxes. Corporate tax receipts can be supported by large exporters even while domestic SMEs lose margin.

This creates the **nominal tax buoyancy trap**.

Higher receipts improve the headline budget and encourage new spending promises. But part of the revenue increase may be a tax on inflation rather than evidence of stronger real capacity.

If policymakers treat every nominal revenue surprise as permanent structural room, they commit the proceeds before learning whether the underlying real economy can sustain them.

The correct test is not simply whether tax revenue rises.

It is whether tax revenue rises faster than:

- debt service;
- social-security cost;
- local transfers;
- relief programs;
- the inflation-linked cost of providing public services.

Under the official FY2029 cases, tax revenue rises significantly. The balance gap still widens.

## 4.15 The investor base is changing at the margin

Japan's ability to fund itself has historically depended on a large domestic investor base and the Bank of Japan.

At the end of 2025, the Ministry of Finance's preliminary holder breakdown showed:

| Holder | Share of JGBs and T-Bills |
|---|---:|
| Bank of Japan | 43.1% |
| Banks | 16.3% |
| Insurance companies | 14.0% |
| Overseas | 12.8% |
| Public pensions | 6.1% |
| Pension funds | 2.6% |
| Households | 1.6% |
| Others and general government | remainder |

The BOJ's share had fallen from 47.4% at the end of FY2023 to 43.1% at the end of 2025. Insurance-company holdings also declined. Public-pension and household shares increased, but from much smaller bases.[^mof-holder-table]

The holder percentages understate foreign influence over price formation.

At the end of December 2025, foreign investors held only 6.8% of JGBs excluding Treasury bills, but they accounted for 47.2% of secondary-market spot transactions and 72.7% of futures trading. Including Treasury bills, their holdings totaled approximately ¥149 trillion, or 12.8% of the market.[^mof-foreign-investors]

This is the **foreign marginal-price** mechanism.

Foreign investors do not need to own most of the debt stock to affect the yield at which new issuance clears. Active participation in the secondary and futures markets can shape the price observed by domestic institutions and primary dealers.

The distinction is crucial:

```text
ownership of the stock
≠ control of the marginal price
```

Japan remains domestically funded in a broad sense. It is becoming more internationally priced at the margin.

## 4.16 Higher yields attract buyers by making the fiscal problem more expensive

A rising yield has two opposite effects.

It improves the investment case for JGBs. Banks, pensions, insurers, households, and foreign investors receive more compensation. Domestic institutions that avoided bonds at near-zero yields may return. Foreign investors may find hedged yen yields attractive relative to other developed-market sovereigns.

At the same time, the higher yield is the source of the future fiscal burden.

This creates a feedback contradiction:

```text
higher yield
→ stronger investor demand
→ better auction absorption
but
→ higher refinancing cost
→ larger future debt service
```

A successful auction therefore does not prove that the fiscal problem is solved.

It proves that the security cleared at a price investors accepted.

The sovereign can remain fully funded while the market steadily raises the cost of that funding.

This is why failed auctions are not required for fiscal compression. The thesis would become much more severe if auctions repeatedly failed or if structural foreign withdrawal appeared. The current argument operates before that point.

The system functions. The price changes.

## 4.17 Shortening maturity shifts cost into future rollover risk

The FY2026 issuance plan reduces supply in parts of the super-long sector and increases issuance in maturities of ten years or less. The Ministry cites weaker life-insurance demand for thirty- and forty-year bonds and greater dependence on foreign demand in the super-long zone.[^mof-issuance-plan]

This is rational debt management.

Issuing where demand is strongest can reduce immediate funding cost and stabilize auctions. It avoids forcing excessive long-duration supply into a market demanding a large premium.

It also shortens the flow-based maturity of new issuance and increases future rollover exposure.

The Ministry's own Cost-at-Risk analysis reports that both expected cost and risk rose between the FY2025 initial plan and the FY2026 plan because of the higher and more volatile rate environment. It further notes that the maturity revisions alone reduced modeled cost while increasing modeled risk because average maturity was shortened.[^mof-car]

This is the maturity-compression box:

```text
weak long-end demand
→ reduce super-long issuance
→ issue more short and medium debt
→ lower immediate term premium
→ bring the next refinancing date closer
```

Japan buys current market stability with future rollover frequency.

There is no free maturity choice.

Long issuance locks in expensive rates for longer. Short issuance keeps current cost lower but exposes the budget sooner if rates remain high.

## 4.18 The long end is pricing institutional credibility, not only inflation

In July 2026, the ten-year JGB yield moved close to 3%, while the twenty- and thirty-year sectors reached levels near 4% during periods of renewed inflation and fiscal concern.[^reuters-yields-july]

Part of that move reflected global rates and energy-driven inflation.

Part reflected Japan-specific institutional pricing.

Investors were evaluating:

- the scale of future fiscal spending;
- the government's commitment to debt-ratio reduction;
- possible pressure on the BOJ to maintain low rates;
- the credibility of central-bank independence;
- the probability of emergency bond buying if yields rose further.

Former BOJ policy-board member Seiji Adachi argued that the government would probably view the 3%–3.5% area on the ten-year yield as a line of defense because yields above that level could weaken the assumption that nominal growth will exceed the long-term rate and raise doubts about fiscal sustainability.[^reuters-adachi]

That is not an official yield target.

It is useful because it identifies the political boundary.

If the BOJ raises short rates to control inflation while the government pressures it to contain long yields, Japan moves toward a divided policy regime:

- market discipline for firms and households;
- partial rate protection for the sovereign.

That conflict belongs primarily to the next chapter. Its fiscal origin is visible here.

The debt stock makes the long end politically sensitive. The long end then limits how freely monetary policy can operate.

## 4.19 Why monetary sovereignty does not remove the constraint

The strongest counterargument is simple:

Japan issues debt in its own currency. The BOJ can buy JGBs. Therefore, the government cannot be forced to default in the way a foreign-currency borrower can.

The first two statements are correct. The conclusion is incomplete.

Monetary sovereignty changes the failure mode.

It moves the adjustment away from an involuntary shortage of yen and toward:

- currency depreciation;
- inflation;
- reduced real household purchasing power;
- financial repression;
- lower real returns for savers;
- central-bank balance-sheet expansion;
- political conflict over interest rates;
- redistribution between borrowers, savers, taxpayers, and asset owners.

The consolidated state can create yen. It cannot create imported energy, food, labor, medical staff, or foreign purchasing power.

If the BOJ absorbs more JGBs to suppress yields while fiscal policy remains expansionary, the immediate funding constraint is relaxed. The possible cost appears elsewhere through the yen and inflation expectations.

If the BOJ refuses to suppress yields, the cost appears directly in the budget through rollover.

The choice is not between constraint and no constraint.

It is between transmission channels.

## 4.20 Why domestic ownership does not remove the constraint

A second counterargument is that interest paid to domestic institutions remains inside Japan.

Again, the observation is correct but incomplete.

Interest payments to banks, insurers, pensions, and households become income for those sectors. In a consolidated national accounting sense, part of the burden is a transfer within the country rather than a net resource loss to foreigners.

The transfer still matters.

Higher interest expenditure must be financed through some combination of:

- taxes;
- lower spending elsewhere;
- additional borrowing;
- inflation;
- central-bank accommodation.

The distribution also changes.

Bondholders receive more income. Taxpayers and users of public services bear the financing cost. Wealthier households and institutions own more financial assets than low-income households. The transfer can therefore widen distributional differences even when it remains domestic.

Domestic ownership reduces external vulnerability. It does not preserve fiscal flexibility.

---

## 4.21 What would actually weaken this thesis

The debt-compression argument should lose confidence if several developments persist together.

1. **Broad nominal growth remains above the effective refinancing rate.**  
   Not for one year, but long enough to lower the debt ratio and expand tax capacity without generating equally large indexed spending.

2. **The full fiscal balance improves, not only the primary balance.**  
   Interest increases would need to be offset by durable revenue or expenditure reform.

3. **Permanent relief programs are funded.**  
   Temporary tax cuts and income benefits would need explicit, credible, lasting offsets.

4. **JGB yields stabilize despite reduced BOJ absorption.**  
   A durable private investor base would have to absorb issuance without an increasing fiscal-risk premium.

5. **The weighted-average coupon stops rising.**  
   This would require lower market yields, substantially stronger cash redemption, or a change in issuance needs.

6. **Social-security growth is contained without degrading access or shifting excessive cost to households.**

7. **Productivity gains broaden the tax base.**  
   AI, automation, advanced manufacturing, and energy investment would need to raise domestic wages and service productivity rather than remain concentrated in a small set of firms.

Any one of these is possible.

The thesis concerns the probability that enough of them occur together before rollover tightens the budget.

---

## 4.22 Base case, upside case, and stress case

### Base case: managed rollover compression

The government continues issuing successfully. The BOJ normalizes gradually. The weighted-average coupon rises with a lag. National debt service reaches the ¥40 trillion area around FY2029. Supplementary budgets and targeted household support continue. JGB auctions remain functional, but yields contain a larger inflation and fiscal premium.

The state does not lose funding access.

It loses discretionary room.

### Upside case: growth arrives before the reset dominates

Nominal and real growth broaden. Productivity gains raise wages, corporate investment, and tax revenue. Inflation stabilizes without requiring a large term premium. The BOJ can normalize without destabilizing the long end. Permanent spending commitments receive durable funding.

Under this case, the debt ratio falls and the increase in interest expense becomes manageable within a larger revenue base.

This is possible. It requires productive growth rather than inflation alone.

### Stress case: the ¥50 trillion threshold arrives early

Long yields remain materially above the official forward-rate path. The yen remains weak, keeping inflation expectations high. Fiscal policy expands through tax relief, benefits, energy support, industrial subsidies, and geopolitical contingencies. More issuance shifts into shorter maturities because super-long demand remains weak.

The Ministry's own sensitivity framework places national debt service near ¥49 trillion in FY2029 under a two-percentage-point upward rate deviation.

In this case, the political pressure for renewed BOJ bond buying rises sharply.

The fiscal problem becomes a monetary problem.

---

## 4.23 Chapter conclusion: the fiscal state is becoming a maintenance state

Japan's debt level is not new.

The interest-rate regime is.

For decades, the government could expand the debt stock while the average coupon declined. The fiscal system benefited from a powerful offset: more principal, but less cost per unit of principal.

That offset has reversed.

The debt stock is still increasing. The average coupon is now rising. Interest expenditure is accelerating. Social-security demand is expanding. Political support programs are becoming harder to reverse. The BOJ is reducing its role as automatic marginal buyer while the market demands higher compensation.

Japan can endure this process for a long time.

Its long maturities, domestic institutions, monetary sovereignty, administrative capacity, and large tax base all extend the timeline.

But endurance is not the same as freedom.

The annual budget increasingly begins with decisions made by the past:

- bonds already issued;
- promises already made;
- benefits already expected;
- care already required;
- support already politically difficult to withdraw.

The state shifts from choosing new priorities to maintaining inherited systems.

That is the fiscal expression of multi-box compression.

The next chapter follows the pressure into the institution expected to manage it: the Bank of Japan. Once debt service becomes sensitive to yields, monetary policy can no longer be evaluated only through inflation and growth. Every rate decision changes the government's future interest bill, every bond-purchase decision changes the yen and market credibility, and every attempt to protect one side of the system tightens another.
