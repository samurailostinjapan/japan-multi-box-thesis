# 4. Debt and Fiscal Capacity

Japan's debt problem should be analyzed through annual debt service and rollover, not only through the gross debt-to-GDP ratio.

Much of the debt stock was issued when interest rates were near zero. That debt does not reprice immediately. It becomes more expensive as bonds mature and are refinanced.

The mechanism is:

```text
old low-cost debt matures
→ refinancing occurs at higher yields
→ annual debt service rises
→ less budget space remains for social spending and relief
→ new borrowing fills the gap
→ future refinancing needs increase
```

The important question is not whether Japan can issue yen debt. It can.

The important question is how much of the annual budget is absorbed by debt service, pensions, healthcare, long-term care, defense, industrial policy, household support, and regional maintenance.

If debt service rises toward the same scale as major social-spending categories, the state is forced into allocation and triage.

A temporary food-tax cut, permanent income benefit, energy subsidy, or emergency corporate program may be politically rational. The problem is timing. These commitments are being added just as the cost of refinancing the existing debt stock rises.

This is the **tax-cut and debt-service collision**.

The risk is not an overnight funding stop. It is the gradual conversion of a flexible budget into a maintenance budget dominated by inherited obligations.
