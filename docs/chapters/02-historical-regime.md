# 2. Historical Stability Regime

Japan's earlier stability rested on a combination of weak wage growth and unusually predictable costs.

Households could tolerate modest income growth because:

- prices rarely changed;
- mortgage costs were extremely low;
- childcare and education costs could be planned;
- savings retained purchasing power;
- long-term household decisions were not repeatedly invalidated by inflation.

This regime allowed income to be converted into opportunity.

A household did not need rapid wage growth if monthly surplus remained predictable enough to fund college savings, extracurricular activities, larger housing, travel, support for children during university, and retirement preparation.

The current regime is different. Rising food, energy, housing, medical, and education costs convert more income into maintenance.

The key shift is therefore not simply from low inflation to higher inflation. It is from **planning confidence** to **cost uncertainty**.

That change weakens fertility, consumption, and intergenerational investment even before household income falls.
