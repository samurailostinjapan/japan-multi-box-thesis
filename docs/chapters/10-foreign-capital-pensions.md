# 10. Foreign Capital and Pensions

Japan does not need foreigners to own most JGBs for foreign investors to influence the price.

The marginal buyer can set the yield at which new debt clears.

As domestic absorption weakens and the BOJ reduces purchases, Japan has stronger incentives to attract foreign bond funds, international asset managers, domestic pensions, insurers, and repatriated Japanese capital.

Higher yields make yen debt more attractive, but the same yields raise the sovereign's financing cost.

Foreign investors may also hedge the currency. Japan can therefore receive bond demand without receiving durable yen support.

Pension repatriation is slower than market pricing.

Markets can react to a headline immediately, while GPIF and other institutions move through governance reviews, maturity proceeds, coupons, allocation bands, and fiduciary requirements.

This creates a timing mismatch.

Domestic reallocation can help absorb supply, but using pension assets for stabilization risks conflict with long-term beneficiary returns.
