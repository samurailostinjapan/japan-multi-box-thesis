# 7. Wages, Bonuses, and Households

Japan's wage debate is often reduced to annual base-pay increases. That misses the structure of household cash flow.

Employers can protect base-pay headlines while adjusting bonuses, overtime, allowances, hiring, promotions, temporary employment, and working hours.

For many households, summer and winter bonuses are not optional luxury income. They are used for larger payments, savings, education, travel, debt reduction, and household replacement purchases.

A smaller bonus can therefore create immediate stress even when monthly base pay rises.

The more important variable is household surplus:

```text
after-tax income
− food
− housing
− energy
− taxes and social premiums
− childcare and education
− transport
− medical costs
− debt payments
= household surplus
```

The thesis argues that middle-class stability depends on the residual.

When the residual shrinks, households cut izakaya visits, alcohol, travel, extracurricular activities, college savings, family formation, and support for adult children.

This is not poverty in the narrow sense. It is **opportunity compression**.

The household can still survive while losing the ability to reproduce its standard of living for the next generation.
